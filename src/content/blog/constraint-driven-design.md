There's a paradox at the heart of creative work: **the more freedom you have, the harder it is to make something great**.

When everything is possible, nothing is chosen. The blank canvas is terrifying precisely because of its infinite potential. But give a designer a single color, or a poet a strict meter, and suddenly clarity emerges from constraint.

## The Grid as Liberation

I spent three years avoiding grid systems. They felt rigid, corporate, bureaucratic — antithetical to the kind of raw, alive work I wanted to make. Then I spent a week studying Swiss typography from the 1950s and everything changed.

The masters of Basel weren't imprisoned by their grids. They were *freed* by them. The grid handled the micro-decisions so the mind could focus on the macro ones. Where does the reader's eye go? What matters most? What can be stripped away?

## Constraints as Concept

The best constraints aren't arbitrary — they emerge from the problem itself.

A mobile interface constrained to a single thumb's reach isn't fighting the user; it's *respecting* them. A writing constraint that forces you to eliminate adjectives isn't punishing you; it's demanding that your nouns do real work.

When I design now, I start by asking: *what are the necessary constraints here?* Not the artificial ones I could impose, but the ones the project itself implies. The answer usually tells me everything about the direction.

## The Permission to Stop

Perhaps the deepest gift of constraints is that they tell you when you're done.

Without boundaries, work expands to fill all available time. With them, you reach completion — a genuine, recognizable endpoint where the work is finished, not abandoned.

That's the thing nobody tells you when you're starting out: done is not the enemy of perfect. Done *is* perfect, when the constraints were right to begin with.
