I published my first tutorial when I barely knew what I was talking about.

It was about CSS flexbox — a topic that now seems almost trivially simple — and I was wrong about three things in it. The comments corrected me within hours. It was mortifying and, also, the best learning experience I'd had in years.

## The Myth of Readiness

We're taught that expertise precedes expression. Learn first, share second. Master the craft, *then* show your work.

But this model misunderstands how knowledge actually forms. Understanding doesn't arrive complete and polished. It accretes, slowly, through application and feedback. The act of explaining something is part of how you understand it.

The teacher learns more than the student. Not because they're smarter, but because articulation forces precision.

## What You Lose by Waiting

Every idea has an expiration date — not in the sense that it stops being true, but in the sense that it stops being *yours to give*.

When you wait until you're certain, you wait until someone else has already said it better. The version of the idea that only you could have expressed, from exactly where you were standing, at exactly that moment — that version is gone.

## The Compound Effect of Small Shares

I have a note from a stranger who said a half-finished essay I wrote in 2022 helped them get through a difficult month. I barely remember writing it. It felt incomplete to me — I never finished the argument I was building toward.

They didn't need the finished argument. They needed exactly that particular halfway-there thing, that honest in-progress artifact of someone working something out.

That's what learning in public actually offers: not expertise, but *process*. And sometimes process is what people need most.

---

*Share the draft. Publish the question. Post the half-formed thing. The alternative is silence, and silence helps no one.*
